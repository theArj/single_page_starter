What Is The Loudness War?

History of the Loudness War

Have you ever listened to song that just amped you up, or made you cry? What was it about the song that moved you so deeply? Was it the beat, the singer, or that blazing guitar solo? Was it the pair of headphones you were listening on? All of these factors contribute to the listening experience. But, as a formally trained audio engineer, with more than ten years of experience, I can tell you that a well-written song, sung by a phenomenal singer, with some of the best musicians on the track can still fall victim to the effects of the "Loudness War".

Before delving into the Loudness War, we first have to go back in history, when music was recorded (or "tracked") using analog gear, such as large format recording consoles with lots of electronics, mechanical reel-to-reel tape machines that captured audio, outboard equipment, such as compressors and limiters, equalizers, etc. This gear modified the sound to create a final mastered track, with the signature "warm" analog quality that the public would buy in the form of vinyl records, 8-track, or cassette tapes.

When computers became more powerful, a new medium began appearing in the early 1980s in the form of digital audio stored on Compact discs (CDs). CDs provided a "cleaner" signal, without the pops and clicks of vinyl records, and could be easily produced and distributed without signal degredation. It was cheaper to record and mix on a computer, since analog tape and gear are expensive, difficult to maintain, and takes quite a bit of room to store. Large studios still use analog outboard gear, but rarely ever a 2-inch reel-to-reel Studer or Otari tape machine. Instead, most studios transitioned to digital audio workstations DAWs, such as Avid Pro Tools or Apple Logic Pro. However, this analog to digital tradeoff comes at a cost.

Audio engineers are notorious for getting technical, discussing dynamic range, dBVU, spatial placement, etc. Bear with me, as I explain these simple concepts with complex-sounding names, and I'll take you on a journey that may cause you never to listen to music the same way. This has its pros and cons, so take the red pill at your own risk!

Digital audio and analog audio have some big differences. First, analog loudness is measured using decibels volume units (dBVU), while digital audio is measured using decibels full scale (dBFS). We'll get to the differences here in a second. Analog gear, such as vacuum tubes on guitar amplifiers, or tape machines, impart harmonics that create a type of saturation that gives it that "warm" thick sound that you hear on most music before the 1990s. Recording engineers would often "drive" a track a decibel (dB) or two over the 0dBVU mark on the meter. Audio engineers can push a kick drum or electric guitar track just a bit to create this "warmth." But this is not so when it comes to digital audio. Digital audio cannot go over 0dbFS. When a digital audio signal hits, or tries to go past the 0dbFS mark on the track's meter, it will create what is called "clipping," which results in a very distorted signal. It is vital to set digital audio levels to prevent clipping the audio. A great way to remember to keep your signal in the healthy range is to remember that the analog 0(dBVU) is equivalent to the digital -18dbFS, with a maximum peak of -6dbFS. This will provide enough headroom for adding effects, such as compression, equilization, reverb, delay, etc. without overloading the master stereo output during the mixing stage. What proper levels also provide is more spatial placement recognition. For example, when you pan the guitar to the left, you will be better able to hear it as such.

Now that we got all of that technical jargon out of the way, let's see how these Loudness War came about. With the introduction of digital audio, record labels noticed that digital audio could be made louder through compression. After all, why should a record label allow their competitor's albums to sound louder than theirs? So, the labels began requesting mastering engineers, who polished the songs and arranged the album, to make albums that sounded louder, and to get it as close to the 0dbFS mark as possible. At first, this may sound harmless. Music always sounds better when played louder, right? Not always...

Many record label executives do not necessarily have on-the-ground experience in the studio, producing, mixing and mastering music. As a business, a record label makes decisions that affect profit, while musicians and studio personnel mostly work towards artistic expression. A successful mastering engineer with 30 years of experience has highly tuned ears, techincal mastery over his or her gear, and knows what it takes to create a hit record from the artistic standpoint. When business executives from record labels stepped in and began making decisions normally relegated to the studio experts, thus began the Loudness War. Think of it as the investors of a private hospital telling the doctors how to treat patients in order to maximize their profits.

Early in the 1980s, the digital waveform of a stereo track contained prominent peaks and dips, indicating broad dynamic range, room for the track to breathe, and much better spatial placement awareness of the elements in the track, such as vocals, instruments, effects, etc. Over time, CDs became louder and louder, leading to what is known as "hypercompression". Despite mastering engineers speaking out about the effects of hypercompression, record labels continued to insist on "louder" tracks. "Remastered" versions of classic albums hit the market. But something awful was happening...

While audio engineers protested behind the scenes, the public was blissfully unaware of the longterm effects of listening to hypercompressed music can cause. Many noticed ear fatigue, but could not explain why. Others were unable to clearly hear instruments that once sat in their own frequency pockets because of the gigantic hypercompressed wall of sound blaring through their earpods or speakers. Others expressed a general dislike for "music today". What is a remedy to this problem?

With the introduction of mp3s and other lossy digital formats that removed data from a digital audio file in favor of a smaller file size, it had an interesting effect. When creating an mp3s from an old CD, the amount of discarded information can be audible. But when creating an mp3 from a hypercompressed CD, the result can actually be closer to an early CD because of the amount of data that mp3s remove from a track containing an unnecessary amount of sound data. Although, this is nowhere near a remedy to the problem of hypercompression and Loudness War.

But hope is not lost! Over the past few years, after much debate, songs are now being mastered with increased dynamic range. With a resurgence in vinyl records among young listeners, comes education in why music sounded so full of life, with lots of punch and clarity on those old records. The music industry has forever changed. With more artists creating tracks at home and bypassing record labels, the artist is more free to choose the medium and platform to release their work. Major streaming services are now normalizing their audio to a more reasonable level. We may have finally ended the Great Loudness War of the 21st century!
